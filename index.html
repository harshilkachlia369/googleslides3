What This Covers

01

Ideation

Ideation is the starting point of every great design. It is the process of generating as many ideas as possible — without judging any of them — to find creative solutions to real problems. Techniques like brainstorming, mind mapping, and SCAMPER help push beyond the obvious and uncover unexpected approaches.

The goal isn't quality at this stage — it's quantity. The more ideas on the table, the higher the chance one of them becomes something truly great. Ideas are sketched, written down, and shared, then filtered into the strongest candidates for the next stage.

  • Brainstorming & mind mapping
  • No idea is a bad idea at this stage
  • Divergent thinking before narrowing down
  • "How Might We" problem framing

2D to 3D

Once ideas are selected, they move from flat sketches on paper into three-dimensional digital models. This is where a drawing gains depth, volume, and real-world scale. 2D sketches define the shape and dimensions of a design; 3D modeling brings that design to life so it can be seen, tested, and refined from every angle.

The transition from 2D to 3D catches errors early — things like clashing parts, wrong proportions, or assembly issues — long before any physical material is used or wasted.

  • Hand sketches define initial geometry
  • 2D CAD drawings add precision and dimensions
  • Extrusion and revolution create 3D solids
  • 3D models enable visualization from all angles
03

Iteration

No design is perfect the first time. Iteration is the process of repeatedly refining a design based on feedback, testing, and new information. Each cycle — build, test, learn, improve — moves the design closer to the best possible version.

Iteration is what separates good design from great design. It removes assumptions and replaces them with evidence. A design might go through dozens of iterations before it is ready — and that is exactly how it should work.

  • Build → Test → Learn → Improve
  • Prototypes used to gather real feedback
  • Failures in iteration are data, not setbacks
  • Each loop makes the design smarter

CAD

Computer-Aided Design (CAD) is the software used to create precise, detailed digital models of a product or part. Tools like Fusion 360, SolidWorks, and AutoCAD let designers define exact dimensions, tolerances, materials, and assemblies with accuracy that hand drawing simply cannot achieve.

CAD is the bridge between a design concept and physical manufacture. A finished CAD model can be sent directly to a 3D printer, CNC machine, or production facility — making it one of the most important skills in modern product design and engineering.

  • Exact dimensions and tolerances defined digitally
  • Tools: Fusion 360, SolidWorks, AutoCAD
  • Enables simulation before physical production
  • Output goes directly to 3D print or manufacture
